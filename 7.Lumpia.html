Lumpia





Origins And Stories

Lumpia has its roots in Chinese cuisine, specifically from the southern province of Fujian, where a similar dish called "lumpia" or "lunpia" is served. Filipino traders and immigrants brought this dish to the Philippines, where it evolved and became a staple in Filipino cuisine.



Traditional Lumpia

Traditional lumpia is a savory dish consisting of a thin wrapper filled with a mixture of vegetables, such as cabbage, carrots, and onions, and sometimes meat or seafood. The filling is wrapped in a lumpia wrapper, typically made from wheat flour, water, and salt, and then deep-fried until crispy and golden brown.

Cultural Significance

Lumpia, both savory and sweet, holds a special place in Filipino cuisine and culture. It's a popular snack or dessert served during special occasions like fiestas, parties, and celebrations.






Key Ingredients

Lumpia

- 1 cup mixed vegetables (e.g., cabbage, carrots, onions)
- 1/2 cup cooked and shredded chicken or pork
- 1/4 cup chopped scallions
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce
- 1 tablespoon oyster sauce (optional)
- Salt and pepper to taste
- 1 package of lumpia wrappers (usually found in the frozen food section or at Asian markets)
- Vegetable oil for frying
